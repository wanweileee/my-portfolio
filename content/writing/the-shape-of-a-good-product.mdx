I have been working alone on small products for a year now. Here are four things I learned.

## Three habits that compounded

**One: write the press release first.** Before the migration, before the schema, before the framework choice — write the sentence you would put in a press release if the thing worked. It clarifies what you are actually trying to make.

**Two: build the seam, not the system.** Most software is interesting at the joints. The internal coherence of a module matters less than how it meets the next one. I spent more time this year designing seams than I spent designing systems, and the work was better for it.

**Three: ship the rough version on Friday.** A finished thing on Friday is worth two unfinished things on Monday. Even if it is small. Even if it is ugly. The momentum compounds.

## One habit I couldn't keep

I tried, for about three months, to keep a daily build log. The theory was that a written record of my thinking would compound into something I could mine later — for posts, for talks, for self-knowledge.

What actually happened: I wrote four entries a week instead of seven, the prose got worse instead of better, and after a while the act of writing the log felt like a small tax on the act of doing the work.

I stopped. The work got better. I'm still not sure what to make of this.

> Maybe the answer is that the habit was right and the format was wrong.

I will try again next year, in a different shape. We'll see.
