On building careful things

A short note on restraint as a product virtue, and why doing less is sometimes the most expensive decision a team can make.

There is a version of every product where you do less and the user gets more.

The trick is not the doing-less — it's noticing which less is which.

The cheap less

Sometimes "less" is just laziness wearing a turtleneck. A team cuts a feature because it's hard, or because nobody has the energy to argue for it. The result feels minimalist for about a week, and then it becomes obvious that something is missing — not because the product is incomplete, but because the thinking is.

The expensive less

The other kind of less is harder. It looks the same from the outside but costs more to make. You build the feature, look at it, understand why it's there, and then — deliberately, with a small ache — remove it.

The most expensive design decision is the one nobody can see.

You can't make this kind of less by accident. It requires a person who has held the full shape of the thing in their head, and decided, in good faith, that the thing is better without a particular part of itself.

What this means in practice

If you're a designer or an engineer who tends toward maximalism, here is a small habit that has changed my work: every time I add a feature, I write down what removing it would cost. Sometimes the answer is "nothing." Most of the time, the answer is "actually, less than I thought."

Then I remove it.

Then I see how the week goes.
