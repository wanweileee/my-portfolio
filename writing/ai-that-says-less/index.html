AI that says less

What happens when you optimise an assistant for silence rather than completion. Some surprises about user trust.

Most AI tooling is loud by default. The product manager in the model's training data really wants you to feel like you got your money's worth — so it fills in the blanks before you've finished thinking, and offers three follow-up suggestions even when the right answer was a single word.

I have been quietly running an experiment for the last six months on what happens if you optimise an assistant for the opposite.

The setup

Two assistants. Same model, same context, same task. The only difference is the prompt: one is told to be helpful, the other is told to be quiet.

A useful assistant is one you trust to say nothing.

The "quiet" prompt is pretty small. It says: prefer one sentence to three. Ask a clarifying question if you're not sure. Decline confidently if you don't know. Treat the user's attention as a finite resource.

What happened

Two things, both of them surprising:

  1. Users finished more tasks with the quiet assistant. Not by a huge margin — maybe 8% — but consistently, across three task categories.
  2. Users reported trusting the quiet assistant more, even when its answers were less complete. A representative quote: "It feels like it knows when it doesn't know."

The second one is the interesting one. We have spent a decade training models to never say "I don't know." We may have overcorrected.

What I'm taking from it

For the products I'm shipping now, the working rule is: if the model isn't at least 80% sure, it should say less, not more. If the user wants more, they can ask.

This is, I think, an opinion. It might also be a discipline.
