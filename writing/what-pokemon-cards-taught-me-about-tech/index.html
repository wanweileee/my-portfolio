What Pokémon cards taught me about tech

A short note on how studying Pokémon card population reports turned out to be the same skill as reading the AI news cycle. Show up, pay attention, build a thesis.

Pokémon cards have outperformed the S&P 500 over the last decade. Not the hype cards, not the TikTok-pumped chase rares, the right cards. Sealed vintage boxes. First edition holos. Iconic art. The cards that collectors with a thesis were quietly buying while everyone else was chasing whatever was trending that month.

That gap, between the people who chase and the people who study, is the thing that hooked me about collecting. The market does not reward enthusiasm. It rewards research. You learn to read population reports, track auction comps, understand print runs, watch which sets are aging well and which are flooding the market. The collectors who win are not the ones who love Pokémon the most. They are the ones who pay attention the most.

The market does not reward enthusiasm. It rewards research.

The same skill, different table

Tech works the same way. Frameworks spike and crash. Every few months there is a new model, a new tool, a new "this changes everything." Most of it is noise. Some of it is the next thing. Telling the difference is the entire skill, and the only way to develop it is the same way you develop an eye for cards: by paying attention every day, not just when something goes viral.

That is why I read AI news the way I check pop reports. Not because every headline matters, but because the pattern only shows up when you are watching consistently. You start to see which research lines are compounding, which tools are quietly becoming infrastructure, which "breakthroughs" are repackaged versions of last year's idea. You learn what is durable and what is hype, and you learn it before the rest of the market does.

What stuck

Collecting taught me that the people who win are not the ones with the most money or the most luck. They are the ones who showed up, paid attention, and built a thesis while everyone else was chasing the trend. I try to do the same with software. Stay curious, stay current, and bet on the things that will still matter when the hype cycle moves on.
